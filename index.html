01Define your recurring publication

Cadence, format, and your next three subject lines written as specific promises. The subject line is the whole open decision.

A promise names what the reader will get. A tease hides it. "What the supermarket markup actually pays for" beats "This week's thoughts." Write three you could actually send.
Example"Why your favorite clean-label brand quietly reformulated" · "What the supermarket markup actually pays for" · "The co-packer minimum that decides what's on the shelf"
02Build your list-building mechanics

The end-of-piece ask, one lead magnet done the bootstrap way (if a useful extension exists), and the owned real estate that will carry your subscribe path.

The end-of-piece subscribe ask

The highest-leverage tactic. One clear invitation at the end of every piece, at peak interest. One field, one sentence, no second page.

Example"If this was useful, I write one of these most weeks — the next one is on what 'natural flavor' actually means. Subscribe here."

Your lead magnet — done the bootstrap way

Optional but high-leverage. A genuinely useful free resource that's a natural extension of a specific piece — a checklist, template, or data appendix. They get it, they're on the list, nothing automated fires behind it except the welcome sequence. If no useful extension exists yet, leave this blank rather than inventing a gated PDF.

The bootstrap test: would the reader genuinely want this after reading the piece, and does nothing automated fire behind it? If you can't answer yes to both, it's the corporate version — skip it.
ExampleA one-page "clean-label decoder" translating misleading label terms. It's the natural next thing after my most-read essay on label language. Reader gives an email, gets the decoder, lands on the list — only the welcome sequence fires.
Owned real estate

The subscribe path should live everywhere you already are. Check each place you'll put it.

03Write your welcome sequence

The actual three emails — not a plan to write them later. And the automations you're explicitly choosing not to build.

The three-email welcome sequence

Hand-written once, sent automatically to every new subscriber. Write a subject line and the core of each email's body.

What you're explicitly NOT building

The trap is premature automation. Check each automation you're committing to not build until you can name a specific problem it would solve.

ExampleNone of these would produce more value than the time to design and maintain them. Until the publication produces replies and the list grows on its own, there's no specific problem any of them would solve. I'll revisit only when I can name that problem with specifics.
04Set your reply-rate baseline

The single metric. Measure it on your last few sends, and decide what you'll change based on it.

Measure it

Genuine human replies (not auto-responses, not unsubscribes) divided by emails delivered. At your scale, 1–2% is excellent, 3% is rare. If you haven't sent yet, set your first-month target instead.

If a send spikes replies, write more like it. If replies fall toward zero, the writing is the problem — name what you'll do about it.
ExampleMy two highest-reply sends were both first-person pieces about a specific operational surprise. I'll write more of those and fewer roundup-style notes. If reply rate falls below 1% for three straight sends, I'll assume the writing has gone generic and reset.
